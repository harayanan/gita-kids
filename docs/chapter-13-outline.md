# Chapter 13 — Kshetra-Kshetrajna Vibhaga Yoga (The Yoga of the Field and the Knower of the Field)

## Overview
- **Verses:** 35 (this edition uses the recension that opens with Arjuna's question as verse 1)
- **Folk Art Style:** Pattachitra (Odisha)
- **Central Theme:** The difference between the **field** (kshetra — the body, mind, senses, the whole changing world of nature, prakriti) and the **knower of the field** (kshetrajna — the conscious Self, purusha, the witness who is aware of it all). Krishna is the one Knower seated in every field. True knowledge is telling the changing field apart from the unchanging Self that knows it; that knowledge leads to liberation.
- **Arc:** Arjuna asks what the field, the knower, knowledge, and the knowable are (1) → Krishna defines field and knower, and names himself the Knower in all fields (2–6) → what counts as the field's parts and what counts as true knowledge — the list of virtues (7–12) → the knowable: the supreme Brahman, beyond and within all (13–19) → prakriti and purusha, how the Self seems caught in nature yet is the untouched witness (20–26) → seeing the one Self equally in all, and that nature alone acts while the Self is actionless (27–35).

## Philosophical Orientation
Advaita Vedanta (Shankara's non-dualism) — this is one of the most directly non-dual chapters:
- The kshetrajna (knower) is the Atman; "Know Me as the Knower of the field in all fields" (13.3) identifies that Self with Brahman/Krishna — the same one Self in every body.
- The knowable (13.13–18) is nirguna Brahman: "neither being nor non-being," "without senses yet shining through all senses," "undivided yet appearing divided in beings," "the light of all lights, beyond darkness, seated in the hearts of all." Render this carefully and simply for children — it is the heart of Advaita.
- "He sees truly who sees the one supreme Lord dwelling equally in all beings, the undying within the dying" (13.28) and "all actions are done by prakriti alone, the Self is actionless" (13.30–32) — the witness-Self is untouched, like space that nothing can stain (13.33) and like the one sun lighting the whole world (13.34).
- Krishna speaks AS Brahman/the Self, not as a separate deity. Keep theology light, concrete, and child-friendly — use the chapter's own images (space/akasha, the sun, the field and its knower) rather than abstract lecture.

## Recurring Modern Characters (Pattachitra / Odisha setting)
Pattachitra is the scroll-and-cloth painting tradition of Odisha (Raghurajpur, Puri). Use these for the ~30% modern stories so the chapter stays cohesive:
- **Aarav** — an 11-year-old boy in Puri, Odisha (established in Chapter 3), curious and active; here he wrestles with the chapter's big question: which part of me is really "me"?
- **Dadu** — Aarav's grandfather, a retired fisherman and practical philosopher who teaches through everyday things (a boat, a field, the sea, a lamp).
- The chapter's central word is **kshetra = "field."** A literal farmer's paddy field outside Puri is a perfect anchor — introduce **Hari Uncle**, a rice farmer, for stories about the field that is sown, grows, and is harvested while the farmer who knows it stays the same.

Vary settings beyond this family where useful (a Pattachitra painter's veranda in Raghurajpur, the Puri seashore, a paddy field, the Jagannath temple lanes). Earlier chapters were flagged in QA for over-using one character pair — do not repeat that.

## Story Mix
~70% mythological (≈24 verses), ~30% modern Indian (≈11 verses). Suggested modern verses: **1, 2, 7, 10, 21, 27, 30, 33, 34, 35** plus one more as needed. The abstract teaching suits both: modern stories with Aarav/Dadu/Hari Uncle for the body-vs-Self and field-vs-knower ideas; mythological stories drawn from sages who realised the Self — Yajnavalkya and Maitreyi/Janaka (Brihadaranyaka), Ashtavakra and King Janaka, the sage Kapila (founder of Sankhya, fitting for prakriti/purusha), Uddalaka and Shvetaketu, Nachiketa, the swan/parable of the two birds on one tree (Mundaka Upanishad), and the Mahabharata frame. Do not reuse the same tale twice.

## Verse-by-Verse Plan

### Section 1: The Field and Its Knower (Verses 1–6)
| Verse | Speaker | Teaching | Story Type | Story Concept |
|-------|---------|----------|------------|---------------|
| 1 | arjuna | "I wish to know prakriti and purusha, the field and the knower of the field, knowledge and the knowable, O Keshava" | Modern | Aarav stares at his reflection in the Puri sea and asks Dadu, "Which part is really me — my body, my thoughts, or the one watching them?" — the same question Arjuna asks |
| 2 | krishna | "This body, Arjuna, is called the field (kṣetra); the one who knows it is called the knower of the field (kṣetrajña)" | Modern | Hari Uncle's paddy field: the field is the soil that is sown and reaped; Hari, who plants and watches it change, is the knower who stays the same — body and Self in one picture |
| 3 | krishna | "Know Me as the Knower of the field in all fields; this knowledge of field and knower I deem true knowledge" | Mythological | A sage teaching that the same awareness looks out of every creature's eyes — one Knower in countless fields, like one moon reflected in many pots of water |
| 4 | krishna | "Hear briefly what the field is, its nature and changes, and who the knower is and his power" | Mythological | A guru promising his students a clear, short map of the whole mystery before unfolding it |
| 5 | krishna | "Sages have sung this in many ways, in many hymns, and in the reasoned verses about Brahman" | Mythological | A gathering of ancient rishis, each describing the same truth in his own words and song — many voices, one truth |
| 6 | krishna | "The great elements, ego, intellect, the unmanifest, the ten senses and the mind, and the five sense-objects — these make up the field" | Mythological | A sage laying out, like pieces of a great puzzle, all the parts that together build a living body and its world |

### Section 2: The Field's Changes & What True Knowledge Is (Verses 7–12)
| Verse | Speaker | Teaching | Story Type | Story Concept |
|-------|---------|----------|------------|---------------|
| 7 | krishna | "Desire, hatred, pleasure, pain, the body-aggregate, awareness, and firmness — this is the field with its changes, told in brief" | Modern | Aarav notices how feelings — wanting, disliking, joy, ache — come and go across him like weather across the Puri sky, while he stays to watch them |
| 8 | krishna | "Humility, sincerity, non-violence, patience, uprightness, service of the teacher, purity, steadiness, self-control" — these are knowledge | Mythological | A devoted student serving his guru at the ashram with humility — knowledge shown not as facts but as how one lives |
| 9 | krishna | "Dispassion toward sense-objects, absence of ego, and seeing clearly the pain in birth, death, old age, and sickness" — knowledge | Mythological | Young prince Siddhartha-like wonder is the Buddhist echo; here use a prince-sage who sees the sorrow in clinging and turns toward the changeless |
| 10 | krishna | "Non-attachment, no clinging to son, wife, and home; constant evenness of mind in welcome and unwelcome events" — knowledge | Modern | Dadu stays calm whether the day's catch is huge or empty; Aarav sees that the steady heart, not the size of the catch, is the real treasure |
| 11 | krishna | "Unswerving devotion to Me with single-minded yoga, loving solitary clean places, taking no delight in crowds" — knowledge | Mythological | A sage who leaves the noisy court to worship in a quiet forest grove, his mind fixed on the One |
| 12 | krishna | "Constancy in Self-knowledge, and seeing the goal of true knowledge — this is declared knowledge; all else is ignorance" | Mythological | A teacher contrasting the seeker whose eyes stay on the truth with the one who is forever distracted — knowledge versus forgetting |

| Verse | Speaker | Teaching | Story Type | Story Concept |
|-------|---------|----------|------------|---------------|

| 14 | krishna | "With hands and feet everywhere, eyes and heads and faces everywhere, ears everywhere — That dwells enveloping all" | Mythological | A vision of the cosmic Self whose senses are everywhere because it lives as the seeing in every eye and the hearing in every ear |

| 17 | krishna | "Undivided, yet seeming divided among beings; the sustainer of all, the devourer and the creator" | Mythological | One sky stretching over many villages, seeming to belong to each yet never truly split — the one Self appearing as many |

| 19 | krishna | "Thus the field, knowledge, and the knowable are briefly told; My devotee who knows this becomes fit for My state" | Mythological | A student who finally grasps the whole teaching, his face lighting up — ready now for the highest truth |

### Section 4: Prakriti and Purusha (Verses 20–26)
| Verse | Speaker | Teaching | Story Type | Story Concept |
|-------|---------|----------|------------|---------------|
| 20 | krishna | "Know prakriti and purusha both to be beginningless; the changes and the gunas are born of prakriti" | Mythological | Sage Kapila, founder of Sankhya, teaching his mother Devahuti the two eternal principles — nature and the Self that watches it |
| 21 | krishna | "Prakriti is the cause of body and the organs of action; purusha is the cause of the experience of pleasure and pain" | Modern | Hari Uncle's bullock-cart: the cart and wheels (prakriti) do the moving, but it is the rider (purusha) who feels the bumps and the breeze |
| 22 | krishna | "The purusha seated in prakriti experiences her gunas; clinging to the gunas causes birth in good and evil wombs" | Mythological | A traveller who falls so in love with the scenery that he forgets he is the traveller — attachment that pulls the Self back into the journey of births |
| 23 | krishna | "Within this body is the supreme Purusha — the witness, the permitter, the supporter, the experiencer, the great Lord, the highest Self" | Mythological | The famous image of two birds on one tree (Mundaka): one bird tastes the fruit, the other simply watches — the witnessing supreme Self |
| 24 | krishna | "Whoever thus knows purusha and prakriti with her gunas — however he lives — is not born again" | Mythological | A liberated sage moving freely through the world, untouched, because he has seen which part of him is the deathless witness |
| 25 | krishna | "Some see the Self in the self by meditation; others by the path of knowledge (Sankhya); others by the yoga of action" | Mythological | Three seekers reaching the same mountaintop by three different paths — the meditator, the philosopher, and the karma-yogi |
| 26 | krishna | "Others, not knowing thus, worship having heard it from others; they too cross beyond death, holding fast to what they heard" | Modern | Aarav can't yet grasp the deep teaching, but he trusts Dadu's words and lives by them — and that faithful listening carries him forward too |

| Verse | Speaker | Teaching | Story Type | Story Concept |
|-------|---------|----------|------------|---------------|
