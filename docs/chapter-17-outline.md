# Chapter 17 — Shraddhatraya Vibhaga Yoga (The Yoga of the Three Kinds of Faith)

## Overview
- **Verses:** 28
- **Folk Art Style:** Pichwai (Nathdwara, Rajasthan)
- **Central Theme:** Faith (**shraddha**) shapes a person — "as a person's faith, so is he" (17.3). Krishna shows how faith comes in three kinds, matching the three gunas (sattva, rajas, tamas), and how the same three colours appear in everything we do: the **food** we eat, the **worship** we offer, the **discipline** we keep, and the **giving** we do. He closes with the sacred phrase **"Om Tat Sat"** and teaches that anything done without faith is empty.

## Speaker Map (CRITICAL)
- **Arjuna:** verse 1 (asks about those who worship with faith but set scripture aside — which guna?).
- **Krishna:** verses 2–28.
- `speaker`: arjuna for v1, krishna for v2–28.

## Philosophical Orientation
Advaita Vedanta. Faith and all acts are coloured by the gunas of prakriti; the sattvic is purest and leads toward the Self, but the goal is action offered to the Supreme ("Tat," 17.25) free of selfish reward. "Om Tat Sat" names Brahman. Keep it concrete and everyday (food, giving, kind speech) so children feel the three gunas in daily life; non-judgemental — these are directions to grow, not labels to fear.

## Recurring Modern Characters (Pichwai / Nathdwara, Rajasthan)
- **Meera** (curious girl near the Shrinathji temple), **Dadaji (Mohanlal)** the Pichwai painter, **Dadi (Radha Bai)** who cooks and strings temple garlands — from Ch7.
- The food/charity verses fit warm Nathdwara kitchen, temple-prasad, and giving scenes.

## Story Mix

Vary mythological sources — faith tales; the threefold contrast via different figures; **Karna's selfless charity** (sattvic dāna), a temple yajna, the meaning of "Om Tat Sat" (Upanishadic), a sage of pure speech/mind.
