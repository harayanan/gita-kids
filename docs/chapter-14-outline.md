# Chapter 14 — Gunatraya Vibhaga Yoga (The Yoga of the Three Qualities)

## Overview
- **Verses:** 27
- **Folk Art Style:** Warli (Maharashtra)
- **Central Theme:** The three **gunas** — qualities woven into all of nature — that bind the soul: **sattva** (harmony, clarity, light, calm), **rajas** (passion, restless activity, craving), and **tamas** (dullness, inertia, sleep, delusion). Krishna explains how each binds, how they rise and take turns dominating, their marks, where each leads after death, and how to rise **beyond all three** (gunatita) through steady devotion to reach Brahman.

## Speaker Map (CRITICAL)
- **Krishna:** verses 1–20 and 22–27.
- **Arjuna:** verse 21 (asks the marks of one who has crossed the gunas, how he acts, how he crosses them).
- `speaker`: krishna for 1–20 & 22–27; arjuna for 21.

## Philosophical Orientation
Advaita Vedanta. The gunas belong to prakriti, not the Self; the Atman is the witness beyond them (14.19–20). Liberation = realising one is not the doer (the gunas act) and resting as the changeless Self/Brahman. Krishna is the very ground of Brahman (14.27). Present the gunas concretely and without moralising — as the three "weathers" of every mind — and transcendence as steady, loving equanimity.

## Recurring Modern Characters (Warli / Maharashtra)
- **Jeeva** (Warli child) and **Aaji** (grandmother muralist) from Ch9.
- Vary with the village's rhythms (a calm dawn, a busy market day, a heavy sleepy afternoon) to picture sattva/rajas/tamas.

## Story Mix
~70% mythological (≈19), ~30% modern (≈8: verses **6, 8, 10, 11, 17, 22, 24, 27**). Vary mythological sources — the cosmic seed-and-womb of creation (14.3–4), figures embodying each guna (a serene sage = sattva; an ambitious, restless king = rajas; a heedless, sleep-bound character = tamas), the gunatita sage (Jadabharata, Shuka, or a forest sage indifferent to praise/blame), death-and-rebirth by dominant guna. Do not reuse a tale.
