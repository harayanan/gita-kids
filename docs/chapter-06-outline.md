# Chapter 6 — Dhyana Yoga (The Yoga of Meditation)

## Overview
- **Verses:** 47
- **Folk Art Style:** Madhubani (Mithila, Bihar)
- **Central Theme:** Taming the restless mind through steady practice (abhyasa) and dispassion (vairagya). The true renunciate is the one who acts without clinging to results, not the one who merely stops acting.
- **Climax:** Arjuna objects that the mind is as hard to hold as the wind (6.33–34); Krishna agrees but insists practice and dispassion tame it (6.35–36). Arjuna then asks what becomes of one who tries and fails (6.37–39); Krishna answers that no sincere seeker is ever lost — the fallen yogi is reborn well and continues across lifetimes (6.40–45). The chapter ends declaring the yogi greater than ascetics and scholars, and the devoted yogi highest of all (6.46–47).

## Philosophical Orientation
Advaita Vedanta (Shankara's non-dualism), consistent with the rest of the book:
- The "self that is the friend of the self" (6.5–6) — the lower mind-bound self is lifted by realising the higher Self (Atman), which is Brahman.
- "Sees the Self in all beings and all beings in the Self" (6.29) and "sees Me everywhere" (6.30–31) — the non-dual vision: the same Atman/Brahman in all.
- Meditation withdraws the mind from objects to rest in the Self, not to reach a separate deity. Krishna speaks as Brahman itself.
- Keep theology light and child-friendly; never doctrinaire. Show, through story, that the calm inner Self is the same in everyone.

## Recurring Modern Characters (Madhubani / Mithila setting)
Use these for the ~30% modern stories so the chapter stays cohesive:
- **Ravi** — a restless 9-year-old boy in a Mithila village. His mind jumps everywhere; he cannot sit still. He has a bouncy puppy named **Moti** (the meta summary's "playful puppy" image — use it).
- **Nani (Sarita)** — Ravi's grandmother, a Madhubani painter. She teaches him to settle his mind through small daily practices: painting a single fish, watching his breath, sitting by the pond at dawn. Patient, never scolding.

Vary modern settings beyond this pair where useful (a Bihar classroom, the Kosi riverbank, a potter, a weaver) — avoid repeating the same two characters in every modern story. Earlier chapters were flagged in QA for over-using one character pair; do not repeat that.

## Story Mix

Vary mythological sources — draw from the Mahabharata, Ramayana, Upanishadic teaching tales (Nachiketa, Shvetaketu, Uddalaka), Puranic sages (Vishvamitra, Vasishtha, Patanjali's lineage of meditators), and the Gita's own battlefield frame.
