# Chapter 10 — Vibhuti Yoga (The Yoga of Divine Glories)

## Overview
- **Verses:** 42
- **Folk Art Style:** Kalamkari (Andhra Pradesh)
- **Central Theme:** Krishna reveals his vibhutis — his divine glories. He is the source of everything, and wherever something is the greatest, brightest, or most magnificent of its kind, that splendour is a spark of him. Among the Adityas he is Vishnu, among lights the sun, among mountains Meru, among rivers the Ganga, among birds Garuda, among words the syllable Om. The whole shining universe rests on a single fragment of his being.
- **Arc:** Krishna promises the supreme word and explains why even gods and sages cannot trace his origin (1–7) → the fruit of knowing him as the source: loving devotion, and the inner light he gives (8–11) → Arjuna, overwhelmed, praises Krishna as the Supreme Brahman and begs to hear his glories in full (12–18) → Krishna names his chief glories — the long, radiant litany of vibhutis (19–42), ending: "I stand supporting this entire universe with a single fragment of Myself."

## Philosophical Orientation
Advaita Vedanta (Shankara's non-dualism), consistent with the rest of the book:
- The vibhutis are not many separate gods but the one Brahman shining through the highest, brightest example of each kind. "Whatever is glorious, prosperous, or mighty — know it springs from a fragment of My splendour" (10.41) is the non-dual point: one reality appearing as the many.
- Krishna speaks AS Brahman/the Supreme Self (Purushottama), not as a tribal or local deity. Arjuna's praise (10.12–13) names him paraṁ brahma — the Supreme Brahman.
- "I am the Self seated in the heart of all beings" (10.20) is the chapter's non-dual anchor — the same Atman in all.
- The point of the litany is to train the mind to see the divine in the grandest things and then everywhere; it is a doorway to seeing the One, not nature-worship. Keep it light and wondrous, never doctrinaire.

## Recurring Modern Characters (Kalamkari / Andhra Pradesh setting)
Kalamkari is the pen-and-dye temple-cloth art of Andhra Pradesh (Srikalahasti, Machilipatnam). Use these for the ~30% modern stories so the chapter stays cohesive:
- **Kiran** — a wonder-struck 10-year-old boy in an Andhra Pradesh village who loves the biggest and most magnificent things in nature: the tallest tree, the brightest star, the widest river. Vibhuti Yoga is the perfect chapter for a child who collects superlatives.
- **Thatha (Subba Rao)** — Kiran's grandfather, a Kalamkari artist who paints gods, their vahanas (mounts), and sacred trees onto temple cloth. He helps Kiran see that the greatness Kiran loves is a window to the divine.

To keep the Kalamkari chapters cohesive without over-using one pair, you may also draw on **Meera and her Nani Tara** (the gardener girl and retired-teacher grandmother established in Chapter 15) for an occasional story, and vary settings (a riverbank on the Krishna or Godavari, a temple courtyard, a millet field, the Eastern Ghats). ## Story Mix
~70% mythological (≈30 verses), ~30% modern Indian (≈12 verses). Suggested modern verses: **2, 4, 7, 9, 11, 17, 22, 26, 34, 38, 40, 42**. The vibhuti litany (19–42) is inherently mythological — keep most of it mythological, with only a few modern stories where a child can grasp the "greatest of its kind" idea. Vary mythological sources — Vishnu's avatars, the Adityas and Rudras, Garuda and Vishnu, Meru and the Himalaya, the churning of the ocean (Uchchaihshravas, Airavata, Kamadhenu, Varuni), Prahlada among the Daityas, Skanda (Kartikeya) the general of the gods, Narada among devarshis, Vyasa among sages, the Ganga's descent, Bhishma and Arjuna at Kurukshetra. Do not reuse the same tale twice.
